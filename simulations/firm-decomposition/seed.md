# How Firms and Institutions Can Decompose Themselves for the Post-Web

## Executive Context

Every firm, every institution, every system is a bundle. A bundle of knowledge, skills, processes, and relationships — legible only to the humans inside it. The limit to that legibility is about to end.

AI agents are already reverse-engineering institutions from their public surfaces. Inferring process logic from patterns of behaviour. Reconstructing skill taxonomies from outputs. Building approximate knowledge graphs from published material. They do not need permission. They do not need an org chart. They just need an observable footprint.

The question is not whether any given institution will be decomposed into machine-readable primitives. It is whether it controls the terms.

This has already happened twice in AI alone. Large language models scraped the open web to build products. Generative AI tools ingested creative industries without consent. By the time media organisations tried to invoke copyright protection, the value had already been captured by others. Now generalise this. What happened to media will happen to every firm and institution on the planet — not by a handful of well-funded labs, but by thousands of agentic systems simultaneously, at machine speed.

The firms that move first get sovereign decomposition. The laggards get extracted.

## The Case Study: Outlier Ventures

Outlier Ventures — a venture firm with 12+ years of history and 400+ portfolio investments at the AI-crypto convergence — has spent the past 24 months quietly decomposing itself into its constituent parts. This was not a pivot. It was a consequence of the Post-Web thesis applied to the firm itself.

### What They Saw Coming

Two years ago, three things converged:

1. **Startup creation would become exponential** — more startups, faster, modular, open source, hybrid human-agent teams, focused on features over monolithic proprietary stacks
2. **Hyper-disruptive environment** — the disruptors quickly become the disrupted, creating a proto-founder gig economy
3. **Acceleration becomes self-serve and commoditised** — often serving as purely feeder into funds. The business model would be redundant within two years.

### How They Executed

**They scaled to build statistical relevance.** A portfolio of fifty startups gives you anecdotes. A portfolio of four hundred gives you signal. They needed enough data points across enough categories, stages, and cycles to make their pattern recognition machine-readable — not just the intuition of experienced investors, but structured, queryable, statistically grounded knowledge. Through their accelerator they performed due diligence on over 20,000 startups and helped facilitate over $500 million in investment.

**They scaled to build the deepest founder network.** When you perform due diligence on a founder, you are validating a human being as a credible entrepreneur and domain expert — someone with deep, earned knowledge of a specific industry, technology, or problem space. With an average acceptance rate of sub 2%, these founders are curated from tens of thousands. Whether or not their particular startup succeeds, that validation holds. They remain a credible node. Four hundred founders. Four hundred validated nodes. That is not a portfolio. That is a knowledge graph.

**They scaled to develop skills that could train LLMs.** Through years of accelerating and advising founders, they developed capabilities — how to evaluate a founder, understand the TAM of a startup, theorise and apply token design, construct a thesis for the agentic future by industry, design incentive systems, constitute a decentralised system. They encoded these as skills: initially to help founders, but always understanding these skills would eventually become composable primitives invocable by machines.

**They scaled to understand their own processes well enough to automate them.** At their height, over a hundred staff. Over 24 months, reduced to ten. Ten high-agency people working in fully hybrid human-agent teams. They systematically mapped, formalised, and automated the processes that used to require entire departments — creating agentic workflows that form the backbone of operations. Not by cutting corners. By understanding those processes deeply enough to encode them as composable primitives.

They went from a hundred humans operating a venture firm to ten humans orchestrating a system. And now that the processes are encoded, the skills are composable, and the workflows are agentic — they can scale back up. Not to a hundred people but to thousands of agents.

## The Three Institutional Primitives

### Knowledge Graphs

Everything the institution knows — domain expertise, pattern recognition, accumulated insight, relational understanding. Structured, queryable, machine-navigable graphs with typed relationships and provenance. Not documents sitting in folders.

Key dimensions:
- **Domain mapping**: Key knowledge domains, how they relate, the topology
- **Provenance**: Where did this knowledge come from? How is it maintained?
- **Proprietary vs general**: Which knowledge is genuinely differentiating? Most firms overestimate this.
- **Access model**: Permissioned or open? Fee-gated or freely queryable?

The OV implementation: A Provenance Graph tracking every thesis claim, publication, investment, and organisation as a typed node. A Portfolio Graph with 350+ nodes representing validated founder-nodes across the full Post-Web technology stack, filterable by era (Post Web 2023-2026, Acceleration 2019-2025, Open Metaverse 2020-2023).

### Skills

Everything the institution can actually do — competencies rendered as invocable functions. Not job descriptions. Callable capabilities.

Key dimensions:
- **Capability inventory**: Every discrete competency, categorised
- **Differentiating vs commodity**: Which skills could an agent replicate from public training data?
- **Function signatures**: Inputs, outputs, and constraints for each skill
- **Dependencies**: Which skills depend on other skills or specific knowledge graph access?

The OV implementation: Eight structured concept primitives (SK-001 through SK-008) — Intention Economy, Smart Agent, Conviction Markets, Post Web Stack, Zero to Many, Verifiability Premium, Fluid Collectivism, Agent Experience. Each encoded as agent-consumable markdown files with canonical/emerging/live status.

### Processes

How the institution actually operates — workflows, pipelines, governance models, production methodologies.

Key dimensions:
- **Workflow mapping**: Major operational workflows end to end
- **Decision points**: Which genuinely require human judgment vs which are human-executed out of habit?
- **DAG conversion**: Expressing each process as a directed acyclic graph — an executable workflow agents can run, fork, recombine
- **Parallelisation**: Sequential vs parallelisable steps

The OV implementation: MCP tools (Machine-Callable Protocol) exposing five execution-layer endpoints — Thesis Positions, Conviction Markets, Portfolio Signal, Skill Lookup, Provenance Graph. Currently marked as "building" but architecturally live.

## The API Surface and Radical Openness

Once primitives are mapped, they are exposed as APIs. Design principles:

- **Machine-first**: The primary consumer is an agent, not a human developer. The API matters more than the UI. The agent-navigable surface matters more than the website.
- **Composability**: Primitives must chain with primitives from other sources. The more composable, the more valuable — agents prefer primitives they can recombine.
- **Minimal extraction**: If pricing is too aggressive, agents route around you. Systems tend toward free. Relentless downward pressure on margins.
- **Initiator as Power User**: The way you benefit is not by gatekeeping. It is by being the most sophisticated user of your own substrate.

OV explicitly open-sourced everything — knowledge graphs, skills, APIs, ebooks, skill files — under creative commons. Anyone can take it, fork it, build a competing fund on top of it. The Post-Web does not belong to Outlier Ventures. It is a structural transition.

"Radical openness is not altruism. It is the business model that agents cannot disintermediate."

## Operating Principles Scorecard

Institutions should be scored against these Post-Web operating principles (RAG assessment):

1. **Intention, Not Attention** — Optimised for executing intent or capturing attention?
2. **Machine-First Surfaces** — Products designed for agent consumption or only human browsers?
3. **Minimal Extraction** — Would agents route around your pricing?
4. **Tend Towards Free** — Designed for commoditisation pressure or fighting it?
5. **Initiator as Power User** — Positioned to benefit through competence or through control?
6. **Cypherpunk Native** — User data sovereign? Exit rights? Cryptographic proof?

## Simulation Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Venture Capital Firm Decomposition
Model a traditional venture capital firm (50 staff, $500M AUM, 200 portfolio companies) attempting self-decomposition following the OV playbook. Staff reduction timeline, knowledge graph construction difficulty, skill encoding challenges, process automation barriers. How do LPs react? How do portfolio companies respond? What happens to deal flow when pattern recognition becomes machine-readable?

### Scenario 2: University Decomposition
A major research university (10,000 faculty, centuries of accumulated knowledge, complex governance) attempts to decompose its research knowledge, teaching skills, and administrative processes into machine-readable primitives. How do faculty respond — resistance, collaboration, flight? What happens to the university's moat when its knowledge graph is open? How do students interact with skill primitives directly vs attending courses?

### Scenario 3: Bank Decomposition
A mid-tier commercial bank decomposes its credit assessment knowledge, financial product skills, and compliance processes. Regulatory implications. Customer trust dynamics. How quickly do agents approximate the bank's lending decisions from its observable patterns if it does NOT decompose itself? Comparison: self-decomposed bank vs extracted bank vs bank that does nothing.

### Scenario 4: Media Company Decomposition
A legacy media company — already partially extracted by LLMs training on its content — attempts retroactive decomposition. Content knowledge graphs, editorial skills, production processes exposed as agent-consumable APIs. Can you retroactively control the terms after extraction has begun?

### Scenario 5: The Refusal Scenario
Model what happens to a consortium of firms that explicitly refuse to decompose — investing instead in legal protection, paywalls, and IP enforcement. How quickly do agents approximate their offerings from external observation? How does market share shift to decomposed competitors? At what point does the refusal strategy become untenable?

## Key Variables for Agent Simulation

- Institutional size, age, and complexity
- Knowledge proprietary index (how much is genuinely unique vs commodity)
- Staff culture and change readiness
- Competitive pressure and market dynamics
- Regulatory environment and compliance requirements
- Agent sophistication and reverse-engineering capability
- Customer/stakeholder trust dynamics
- Capital available for transition
- Leadership conviction and decomposition timeline

## Expected Dynamics

The simulation should reveal: optimal decomposition sequencing (knowledge first? skills first? processes first?); the relationship between institutional age and decomposition difficulty; whether radical openness genuinely creates power-user advantage or simply accelerates commoditisation; how fast agents approximate undecomposed institutions; whether partial decomposition (some primitives open, some gated) is stable or unstable; and the social dynamics of staff reduction during decomposition — coalition formation, resistance patterns, talent migration to decomposed competitors.

# How Existing Token Economies Can Adapt to the Post-Web

## Executive Context

The global token economy — encompassing DeFi protocols, Layer 1 and Layer 2 blockchains, DAOs, NFT platforms, and thousands of utility token projects — faces a structural crisis. The original promise of tokens as coordination mechanisms for open-source infrastructure has collapsed into a speculation-dominated attention economy. Meme coins, pump-and-dump cycles, and token launches optimised for marketing rather than utility have captured the entire landscape. The market mechanisms have no way to distinguish speculation from contribution or consumption.

Meanwhile, the most consequential technological transition in decades — the rise of AI agents as primary internet users — has no native financial primitive capable of funding it properly. Equity is too slow and cumbersome. Tokens collapsed into casino instruments. A gap exists between the coordination problem and the coordination mechanisms available.

This simulation explores what happens when existing token economies attempt to transition from attention-economy instruments to Post-Web conviction-based coordination mechanisms.

## The Current State: Attention Economy Capture

Bitcoin and Ethereum demonstrated that tokens could give people direct financial incentive to collaborate on building open-source code and shared infrastructure — something neither equity nor goodwill had managed at scale. The insight was correct. But without any mechanism to constrain speculation, the incentive structure inverted.

Participants made more from trading the token than building for it. Capital flooded toward attention rather than genuine innovation. Token price detached from utility entirely. Everything became a meme coin. The entire token landscape was captured by the attention economy, not the intention economy.

Prediction markets, token launches, and DAO votes are all optimised for prediction — point-in-time bets that reward speed, size, and information advantage. They do not reward sustained commitment. They generate no productive output.

### Structural Failures

1. **Static token design**: Previous generations of tokens were fundamentally static — fixed supply, predetermined schedules, unchanging incentive structures. A static token can price access but cannot govern behaviour.

2. **No commitment mechanism**: Existing tokens treat all holders identically regardless of duration, contribution, or conviction. A day-trader and a core contributor hold the same instrument.

3. **Speculation-participation collapse**: When speculation and participation are collapsed into the same instrument, speculation inevitably dominates. The casino swallows the coordination layer.

4. **No Minimum Viable Economy**: Tokens launched before demonstrating demand. Token incentives without MVE collapse post-subsidies. This is a strong empirical pattern observed across 400+ portfolio investments.

## The Post-Web Alternative: Intention Economy

The Post-Web thesis proposes a structural shift: from an internet optimised for capturing human attention to one optimised for executing human and machine intent. Web evolution proceeds as: Web1 (Read) → Web2 (Read + Write) → Web3 (Read + Write + Own) → Post-Web (Read + Write + Own + Delegate).

In the Intention Economy, AI agents act on behalf of users. They don't browse, scroll, or respond to advertising. They query, transact, and compose. This changes the fundamental economics: attention-based business models dissolve when the "user" is an agent optimising for outcomes.

### Dynamic Tokens as Constitutional Enforcement

Post-Web tokens are dynamic — they respond to system state, adapt to agent behaviour, and reweight incentives in real time. A static token can price access. A dynamic token can govern behaviour. The token translates constitutional values into game theory. Without the token, the constitution is aspirational. With it, the constitution becomes executable.

Token functions in the Post-Web:
- **Incentive alignment** — aligning agent behaviour with constitutional principles
- **Accountability** — staking and slashing for agent and operator behaviour
- **Governance automation** — coordinating activity through token-mediated protocols
- **Ownership and access** — rights over agents, infrastructure, data, revenue sharing

## Conviction Markets: The New Primitive

In a Conviction Market, you don't bet on what will happen — you fund what should be built, and you own a share of what gets built. This is categorically different from prediction: conviction is a committed belief that a problem matters enough to act on — not just to profit from its resolution, but to work toward it and own a share of what emerges.

### Architecture

**The Productive Layer** — where conviction lives. Four core roles:
- **Curators** identify and frame problems worth solving, staking reputation and capital
- **Sponsors** provide capital committed in tranches, unlocking progressively against milestones
- **Contributors** are builders — developers, researchers, designers, and AI agents — who commit to problem stacks
- **Verifiers** assess whether submitted work meets milestone criteria

**The Speculative Layer** — provides price discovery and liquidity, but as a derivative of conviction, not a substitute for it.

**Participation Tokens** record participation weighted by timing and magnitude of commitment. Early entry alone is not enough — participation weight reflects continuous engagement.

### First Principles
- Commitment over outcome prediction
- Co-owner over contractor
- Systems over startups
- Evidence over claims
- Earn by delivering
- Built in the open

## Simulation Scenarios

### Scenario 1: DeFi Protocol Transition
A major DeFi protocol (comparable to Uniswap or Aave) redesigns its governance token as a conviction instrument. The existing liquidity and community represent significant network effects. How do existing holders respond? Does speculation decrease or simply migrate? How do AI agents interact with the new conviction-weighted governance? What happens to protocol development velocity?

### Scenario 2: Layer 1 Chain Adaptation
An established Layer 1 blockchain attempts to transition its token economics from proof-of-stake validation incentives to a hybrid model incorporating conviction-based resource allocation. Validators, developers, and applications all have different relationships to the token. How does each constituency respond? Does the chain lose developers during transition? What do competing chains do?

### Scenario 3: DAO Treasury Restructuring
A well-funded DAO with a large treasury and active community converts from a simple governance token to a conviction market structure around its problem stacks. Capital allocation shifts from vote-based grants to milestone-gated conviction funding. How does participation change? Do large token holders resist the shift from voting power to conviction weight?

### Scenario 4: Cross-Protocol Contagion
If one major protocol successfully transitions, what happens to the broader ecosystem? Do other protocols follow? Does a new class of "conviction-native" tokens emerge? How do exchanges, wallets, and aggregators adapt? What happens to token projects that don't adapt?

## Key Variables for Agent Simulation

- Token holder distribution (whales vs retail vs builders)
- Protocol revenue and utilisation metrics
- Developer community size and activity
- Competitive landscape and network effects
- Regulatory environment and institutional adoption
- AI agent adoption rate and capability
- Market sentiment and speculation cycles
- Cross-protocol composability and DeFi integration depth

## Expected Dynamics

The simulation should reveal emergent patterns around: resistance from speculation-dependent participants, coalition formation between builders and long-term holders, the speed at which AI agents discover and preference conviction-weighted protocols, competitive dynamics between adapted and non-adapted protocols, and the potential for market bifurcation between attention-economy tokens and intention-economy tokens.
